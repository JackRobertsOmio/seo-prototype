Welcome to Berlin
Step off the train into a city that pulses with creative energy and unburdened reinvention. From the graffitied walls of Kreuzberg to the elegant boulevards of Charlottenburg, Berlin refuses to stay the same. History breathes from every corner—the Brandenburg Gate, the Wall's ghost, the scars and triumphs of the 20th century—yet the city remains radically forward-looking, with world-class techno clubs, cutting-edge galleries, and street art that rewrites itself weekly. Spring brings warmth, longer days, and an explosion of outdoor beer gardens and riverside hangouts. This is a city where you can feel the weight of history and the thrill of tomorrow, all at once.

Museum Island (Museumsinsel)
Five major museums including the Pergamon Museum (Greco-Roman antiquities), Neues Museum (Egyptian bust of Nefertiti), and the German Historical Museum. UNESCO World Heritage site. Allow a full day or split visits.
€20–30 per museum • Mitte district
Brandenburg Gate & Reichstag
Berlin's most iconic monument. The Reichstag dome offers 360° city views (book ahead). Nearby: Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe—a haunting, essential 2,711 concrete stelae.
Free to view gate • €3–5 for dome • Mitte
East Side Gallery
1.3 km stretch of original Berlin Wall, now a dynamic open-air gallery. Over 100 murals by international artists; constantly evolving. Best visited in daylight; walk from Friedrichshain or Kreuzberg.
Free • Friedrichshain
Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe
2,711 concrete stelae of varying heights create a disorienting, deeply moving maze. The underground information center provides historical context. Powerful and humbling.
Free • Mitte
Charlottenburg Palace
Prussia's grand royal residence in the west. Stunning gardens, opulent rooms, and sculpture collections. Quieter than Museum Island; worth a half-day trip.
€12–15 • Charlottenburg district
Pro tip: Buy a Museum Pass Berlin (€30) for 3 consecutive days of unlimited entry to 70+ museums.
Techno Clubs
Berghain (industrial, strict dress code), Watergate (rooftop views on the Spree), Tresor (legendary Friedrichshain club), Sisyphos (outdoor garden). Clubs don't get going until midnight; many run 24+ hours.
€8–15 entry • Various districts
Street Art Tours
Explore Kreuzberg's RAW-Gelände, Friedrichshain murals, and East Side Gallery with knowledgeable guides. Many tours led by local artists who explain the political and creative context.
€15–25 per person • 2–3 hours
Bike Tours & Rentals
Over 1,000 km of dedicated bike lanes. Rent from Call a Bike or similar services. Great for exploring parks like Tiergarten, riding along the Spree, or discovering quieter neighborhoods. Guided tours available.
€10–15 per day rental
Flea Markets & Vintage
Mauerpark Sunday flea market (legendary, crowded). RAW-Gelände market (Friedrichshain, Sundays). Kreuzberg vintage shops and markets. Great for records, vintage clothes, and curiosities.
Free entry • Year-round • Varies by market
Spree River Boat Tours
Scenic cruises past Museum Island, the Reichstag, and Kreuzberg. Several companies offer day tours or sunset cruises. A peaceful way to see the city from the water, especially lovely in spring.
€12–20 • 1–2 hours
Pro tip: Visit Mauerpark on Sunday mornings for the flea market and karaoke in the afternoon—a quintessential Berlin experience.
Potsdam
25 minutes by S-Bahn. Baroque palaces (Sanssouci, Neues Palais), manicured gardens, and lakeside charm. Perfect half-day or full-day escape from the bustle.
€3.50 return • S-Bahn S7
Dresden
2 hours by regional train. Saxon capital with stunning Baroque architecture (Frauenkirche, Zwinger Palace). Elbe River views, ceramics collections, and riverside wine gardens. Plan an overnight or long day.
€25–40 return • Regional trains
Leipzig
1 hour 15 minutes by rail. Arts and culture hub with grand concert halls, the stunning St. Thomas Church, and vibrant creative scene. More intimate than Berlin, less touristy.
€20–35 return • Regional trains
Hamburg
1 hour 45 minutes by InterCity. Germany's port city with canals, fish markets, harbor walks, and a more Nordic vibe. Longer day trip; consider overnight.
€40–60 return • InterCity trains
Szczecin
2 hours by regional train into Poland. Castle Island, Oder River, maritime museums, and Eastern European flavor. Budget-friendly, less crowded, great for off-the-beaten-path travelers.
€15–25 return • Regional trains
Pro tip: Use Omio or BVG for journey planning and tickets. Weekend rail passes offer savings for multiple day trips.
Currywurst
Berlin's iconic street food: grilled pork sausage smothered in curry ketchup. Curry 36 and Curry 61 are the famous rivals. Eaten standing up from a paper tray with a wooden fork.
€3–5 • All over the city
Döner Kebab
Berlin has some of the best döner in Europe. Crispy outside, tender meat, fresh vegetables and sauce in warm pita. Street stalls and small shops throughout Kreuzberg, Neukölln, and beyond.
€4–6 • Every neighborhood
Markthalle Neun (Street Food Thursdays)
Historic market hall in Friedrichshain. Thursday evenings feature international street food vendors, craft beer, live DJs, and a young creative crowd. Festive, casual, excellent for sampling.
€5–12 per dish • Friedrichshain • Thursdays 17:00–23:00
Kreuzberg Food Scene
Multicultural neighborhood packed with Vietnamese, Turkish, Middle Eastern, Asian, and fusion restaurants. Affordable, authentic, no-fuss dining. Walk Kottbusser Tor and Mehringdamm for options.
€7–15 per meal • Kreuzberg
Beer Gardens & Cafes
Spring & summer: Biergärten pop up everywhere. Café culture dominates mornings (strong coffee, pastries). Evening beers at rooftop bars with skyline views (Watergate, Klunkerkranich in Neukölln).
€3–5 beer, €2–4 coffee
Pro tip: Restaurants are expensive and formal. Street food and casual eateries give better value and authentic Berlin flavor. Breakfast (brunch) is sacred on weekends.

Club entry: €8–15. Drinks in clubs: €4–6. Live music venue: €10–25. Flea markets: free entry. Street art tours: €15–25.
Budget-friendly compared to other major capitals
Pro tip: Berlin is significantly cheaper than Paris, London, or Amsterdam. A comfortable day costs €40–60 on a budget, €80–120 mid-range. Free museum hours are rarely offered; focus on free attractions like East Side Gallery and street art.
Kreuzberg
Gritty, bohemian, multicultural. Street art everywhere, squatter history, Turkish & Middle Eastern communities, young crowds, experimental galleries & clubs. Chaotic energy; base yourself here for authentic Berlin.
Budget-friendly, vibrant nightlife
Mitte
Historic center: Museum Island, Brandenburg Gate, Reichstag, Memorial. More polished, touristy, upscale. Touristy but essential. Good restaurants & bars. Can feel crowded.
Touristy, central, pricier
Friedrichshain
East Berlin, artistic edge, RAW-Gelände (concert venue & cultural space), East Side Gallery, Markthalle Neun. Young, creative, post-industrial vibe. Clubs and late-night culture dominate.
Nightlife hub, street art, cool cafes
Prenzlauer Berg
Charming, gentrified, cafes & galleries. Boutique shops, Sunday flea markets, craft beer bars. Less edgy than Kreuzberg or Friedrichshain; more polished, Instagram-friendly. Good base for independent travelers.
Trendy, friendly, mid-range prices
Neukölln
Up-and-coming, diverse, street-food hub. Rap culture, vinyl shops, small galleries, adventurous food scene. Less crowded than Kreuzberg. Edgy, creative, still affordable.
Emerging arts scene, budget-friendly
Charlottenburg
West Berlin's grand residential district. Charlottenburg Palace, green spaces, upscale shopping (Ku'damm boulevard). Quieter, more bourgeois, elegant gardens. Better for a day trip than overnight stay.
Palaces & parks, quieter vibe
Pro tip: Stay in Kreuzberg, Friedrichshain, or Prenzlauer Berg for authentic Berlin. Mitte is convenient but touristy. Neighborhoods are connected by excellent U-Bahn & S-Bahn; easy to explore.

Spring Season Tips
March–May is peak season. Book 2–4 weeks ahead for good options. Prices rise. Avoid peak Easter week if budget-conscious. Long weekends fill up fast.
Plan ahead for spring travel
Pro tip: Kreuzberg and Friedrichshain offer the best mix of budget and authenticity. Mitte is convenient but pricier. Check Booking.com, Airbnb, and hostelbookers.com for reviews and last-minute deals.
Overall Safety
Berlin is generally safe for tourists. Street crime is rare. Violent crime uncommon. Like any major city, stay aware in crowded areas. U-Bahn and S-Bahn run safely day and night.
Generally safe
Bike Theft
Bike theft is rampant. Always lock bikes to racks (even for a few minutes). Use sturdy U-locks, not cable locks. Leave valuables out of sight. Bike rentals have insurance; personal bikes less recoverable.
High theft risk
Pickpocketing
Rare but happens in crowded areas: Hauptbahnhof train station, Museum Island, flea markets, club queues. Keep valuables secure. Don't flash expensive items.
Low risk, but stay alert
Late-Night / Nightlife
Clubs and bars are safe. U-Bahn and S-Bahn run 24/7 (Night trains N1–N60 on weekends). Share taxi rides. Avoid walking alone in remote areas very late. Stick to lit, populated streets.
Safe if sensible
Drug Culture
Drugs visible in some neighborhoods (especially Kreuzberg, Friedrichshain) but avoid unsolicited offers. Police don't bother casual users but fines apply. Nightlife venues are generally safe and monitored.
Present, but manageable with caution
Pro tip: Use common sense: watch belongings, avoid isolated alleys, use registered taxis or Uber, keep phone secure in crowds. Berlin is much safer than it looks; the graffiti is art, not danger.
Omio
Book trains across Europe & Germany. Journey planner for Berlin day trips. Compares prices and routes. Essential for Potsdam, Dresden, Leipzig, Hamburg links.
Free • iOS & Android
BVG (Berlin Transport Authority App)
Official app for U-Bahn, S-Bahn, buses, trams. Real-time departures, journey planner, digital ticket purchase (day passes, single tickets). Offline maps available.
Free with paid tickets • iOS & Android
Google Maps
Works perfectly in Berlin. Offline maps downloadable. Great for walking, cycling routes, restaurant reviews, opening hours. Metro integration.
Free • iOS & Android
Lime / Tier
E-scooter rentals. Fun for short distances; cheaper than taxis. Abundant in central neighborhoods. €1 to unlock + €0.15–0.25 per minute.

Free & premium • iOS & Android

€5/month + per-minute • iOS & Android

Spring (March–May): 8–15°C mornings, 15–20°C afternoons. Pack light sweater, cardigan, or hoodie. T-shirts and long-sleeve shirts. Jeans or casual pants. One pair of comfortable walking shoes.
Layering is key for changeable weather
Rain Jacket
Spring brings occasional rain. Pack a compact rain jacket or windbreaker. Umbrella optional (bulky, often not needed). Quick-dry fabrics preferred.
Essential for unpredictable spring
Shoes
Comfortable walking shoes (sneakers, loafers). Berlin involves lots of walking & cycling. Break in shoes before arrival. Consider waterproof or quick-dry materials. Bike theft is high; don't wear valuables on feet.
Durability & comfort over style
Accessories
Lightweight scarf (warmth & style). Sunglasses & hat (spring sun increases). Small backpack or shoulder bag for daily carry. Power bank for extended exploration.
Practical and versatile
Bike-Specific Gear
If renting bikes: padded shorts optional, reflective vest or clip (safety), small lock if exploring remote areas. Call a Bike provides basic locks with rentals.
For cycling comfort & safety
Nightlife & Clubs
Smart casual is fine; many clubs have relaxed dress codes. Dark jeans, nice top, clean shoes. Avoid athletic wear at high-end venues (Berghain, Watergate). Comfortable shoes for standing all night.
Dress codes vary by venue
Pro tip: Pack light; Berlin shops are excellent and prices reasonable. Invest in good shoes—you'll walk 15,000+ steps daily. Bring adapters for EU outlets (Type C, rarely Type F in hostels).
Currency: Euro (€)
Germany uses the Euro. 1 EUR ≈ $1.05–1.10 USD (rates fluctuate). ATMs abundant throughout Berlin. Check with your bank for international withdrawal fees.
Standard EU currency
Card Acceptance
Visa & Mastercard widely accepted at restaurants, shops, hotels, attractions. Amex less common. Contactless payment standard. Smaller cafes & street vendors may request cash.
Broadly accepted, but carry cash backup
Cash
Many places accept cards, but some smaller restaurants, markets, and street vendors prefer cash. Have €50–100 for day-to-day expenses. ATMs free with most international cards.
Bring €50–100 backup cash
Tipping Culture
Tipping expected but more relaxed than US. Restaurants: 5–10% (or round up). Bars: €1–2 per drink or round up. Taxis: 5–10%. Street vendors (currywurst, etc.): optional, round up appreciated.
Less aggressive than US, more than UK
Digital Payments
Google Pay & Apple Pay accepted at most terminals. PayPal for online bookings. Wise or TransferWise good for international transfers if staying longer.
Convenient for contactless payments
Avoiding Fees
Use ATMs from major banks (not airports). Check with your home bank for international withdrawal costs. Exchange currency in advance if poor ATM fees expected. Credit cards for larger purchases (hotels, tours).
Plan ahead to minimize charges
Pro tip: German vendors expect politeness with tipping; saying "Danke" (thanks) with your payment is standard. Many small places still cash-only; always have backup notes. Berlin's payment scene is increasingly digital, but cash still king in old-school cafes.
U-Bahn (Subway)
Underground metro with 10 lines (U1–U9, U55). Covers central & eastern Berlin. Frequent, reliable, runs 24/7 on weekends (restricted hours weekdays). Buy single tickets (€2.80) or passes (7-day €37, 30-day €110).
€2.80 per trip • Frequent service
S-Bahn (Commuter Rail)
Elevated/ground-level rapid transit. Links suburbs, airports (Tegel, BER), and major destinations. Often faster than U-Bahn for longer distances. Same ticketing system as U-Bahn. Connects to regional trains for day trips.
€2.80 per trip • Essential for outer areas
Buses & Trams
Over 350 bus routes, 22 tram lines. Scenic alternatives to metro. Covers areas U-/S-Bahn miss. Night buses (N-series) run 24 hours. Same ticketing system; validates on board.
€2.80 per trip • Covers entire city
Bikes & Bike Rentals
Over 1,000 km of bike lanes. Bike rentals via Call a Bike (€5/month + €0.08–0.12/min), shop rentals (€10–15/day), or Airbnb hosts. Perfect for exploring neighborhoods, riverside, parks. Lock bikes to racks.
€10–15 per day • Best for sightseeing
Taxis & Ride-Sharing
Taxis abundant; phone or hail on street. Uber & Bolt also operate. Flat meter start (€3.90), then per-km. 10 km ≈ €20–25. More expensive than transit but convenient late-night. Fixed airport rates available.
€20–30 for 10 km
Day Trip Trains
Regional trains to Potsdam (S7, 25 min), regional trains to Dresden, Leipzig, Hamburg via Omio or Deutsche Bahn. Weekend rail passes offer savings. Book ahead in peak season.
€3–50 depending on destination
Buying Tickets
BVG app (best for reservations & digital tickets), station ticket machines (English available), or on board (no discount). Validate tickets or risk €60 fine. Weekly/monthly passes cheaper than individual trips if staying 4+ days.
BVG app recommended
Pro tip: Buy a 7-day pass (€37) if staying 4+ days; saves money vs. daily singles. Bikes are fastest for neighborhood exploration. U-/S-Bahn is most reliable for attractions. Always validate tickets to avoid fines. Night buses are reliable and safe for late returns.
